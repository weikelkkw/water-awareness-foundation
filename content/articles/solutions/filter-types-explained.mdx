## Read this before you buy anything

If you only take one thing away: **the NSF/ANSI certification on the box matters more than the brand printed on it.** Two pitchers from two different brands at two different prices, both certified to NSF/ANSI 53 for lead reduction, will remove similar amounts of lead. The brand is mostly paying for marketing.

The big six certifications you should know:

| Certification | What it covers |
|---|---|
| **NSF/ANSI 42** | Aesthetic effects — taste, odor, chlorine. Does **not** cover lead or most health-relevant contaminants. |
| **NSF/ANSI 53** | Health effects — lead, asbestos, cysts, some VOCs, some disinfection byproducts. |
| **NSF/ANSI 58** | Reverse osmosis systems — broad-spectrum reduction including dissolved solids, fluoride, arsenic, PFAS, nitrate. |
| **NSF/ANSI 401** | Emerging contaminants — pharmaceuticals, pesticides, some PFAS. |
| **NSF/ANSI P473** | PFAS-specific reduction. |
| **NSF/ANSI 244** | UV systems for microbial reduction. |

Most cheap pitcher filters are certified only to NSF 42. If you want to remove lead, you need NSF 53. If you want to remove PFAS reliably, you need NSF 53 + P473 *or* an NSF 58 reverse osmosis system. There are no shortcuts on this.

## Activated carbon

How it works: activated carbon — usually carbon block or granular activated carbon (GAC) — has an enormous internal surface area (around 1,000 square meters per gram). Many organic contaminants and some heavy metals adsorb to the carbon's surface as water passes through. Adsorption is non-specific: carbon does a decent job on a wide range of compounds, but not equally on all of them.

What carbon removes reliably (when properly certified):

- Chlorine and chloramine (taste/odor and some chemical reduction)
- Lead — only if certified NSF 53 specifically for lead, and the cartridge has not been overrun
- Many disinfection byproducts (TTHMs, HAAs)
- Many pesticides, herbicides, and industrial VOCs
- Some pharmaceutical residues (NSF 401)
- Some PFAS — but only if certified NSF 53 + P473

What carbon does **not** reliably remove:

- Fluoride (passes straight through)
- Nitrate
- Arsenic
- Most heavy metals other than lead
- Dissolved minerals (this is fine — most are not harmful)
- Microbes (carbon is not a disinfection technology)

Carbon filters have a finite capacity. Once the surface area is saturated, the cartridge has to be replaced. **Manufacturer-stated replacement intervals are designed for average use; if your water is high in chlorine or organic matter, you will exhaust the cartridge earlier.** Many lead-contaminant incidents in the field trace to filters that were past their replacement date.

## Reverse osmosis (RO)

How it works: water is pushed under pressure (typically 40–80 psi) through a semipermeable membrane that excludes essentially all dissolved solids — ions, dissolved gases, and organic molecules larger than a few hundred daltons. The treated water collects in a storage tank; the rejected water flushes down the drain.

What RO removes:

- Lead, copper, chromium-6, arsenic, mercury, and other heavy metals
- Fluoride
- Nitrate
- Most PFAS (>99%)
- Dissolved organic compounds, pesticides, pharmaceutical residues
- Most disinfection byproducts
- Sodium, calcium, magnesium (the "mineral content" of water)

What RO does **not** remove:

- Volatile organic compounds with low molecular weight (some pass the membrane — most RO units include a carbon stage to address this)
- Microbial contamination guarantee — RO membranes reduce microbes significantly but a UV stage is typically added in well-water applications

Tradeoffs:

- **Water waste.** Older RO units rejected 4–5 gallons for every 1 gallon of permeate. Newer units run closer to 1:1.
- **Removes minerals.** RO water is essentially demineralized. Whether you should drink demineralized water long-term is a question with surprisingly limited evidence; most public-health bodies consider it fine for adults with a varied diet. Some RO systems include a "remineralization" stage that adds calcium and magnesium back.
- **Cost.** Under-sink units run $200–$800. Countertop options exist for less. Whole-house RO is generally not necessary or cost-effective.

RO is the most thorough point-of-use option. If you have a specific concern about fluoride, PFAS, arsenic, or chromium-6 — these all require RO (or a niche option below). For lead alone, a properly-certified carbon filter is sufficient and much cheaper.

## Ion exchange

How it works: water passes over a resin bed where specific ions in the water (e.g., lead, calcium) are swapped for ions on the resin (typically sodium or hydrogen). The most familiar application is the household water softener, which exchanges hardness ions (calcium, magnesium) for sodium.

Targeted applications:

- **Water softeners** — exchange Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ for Na⁺. Doesn't make water safer, just reduces scaling on pipes and appliances. Adds modest sodium to drinking water.
- **Nitrate-selective resins** — point-of-use option for high-nitrate agricultural areas. Effective.
- **Chromium-6 specific resins** — used in California, where Cr-6 is regulated separately.
- **PFAS-selective resins** — emerging utility-scale and increasingly residential.

Ion exchange does not address organic contaminants. It's a complement to carbon or RO, not a substitute.

## Distillation

How it works: water is boiled and the steam is collected and condensed. Anything with a higher boiling point than water — dissolved solids, heavy metals, fluoride, nitrate — stays behind.

What it removes: essentially everything non-volatile. Including beneficial minerals.

What it doesn't reliably address: volatile organic compounds with boiling points near water's (some pass through with the steam). Most household distillers include a carbon polishing stage to handle this.

Tradeoffs: slow (typically 1 gallon per several hours), energy-intensive (basically you're boiling all your drinking water), and produces demineralized water. Used to be a common choice for very high-purity applications; for residential drinking water RO has largely displaced it because it's faster and less energy-hungry.

## Ultraviolet (UV)

How it works: water is exposed to UV-C light, which damages the DNA of bacteria, viruses, and protozoa, rendering them unable to reproduce.

What it does: microbial disinfection. Effective against common pathogens (E. coli, Giardia, Cryptosporidium) at adequate dose.

What it does **not** do: remove any chemical contaminant. UV is purely a microbial step.

Where it's appropriate: private well water in regions with surface-water influence, or well water with documented coliform. Not generally necessary for municipal water already disinfected at the plant.

## What about "alkaline," "hydrogen," and "structured" water?

Marketing categories, not regulated ones. Briefly:

- **Alkaline water** — water with elevated pH (typically 8–10), often achieved with an ionizing electrode or added minerals. Claims of meaningful health benefit (cancer prevention, "neutralizing acidity") are not supported by clinical evidence. Your stomach acid is pH 1.5–3.5 and will overwhelm the alkalinity of any glass of water in seconds.
- **Hydrogen water** — water with dissolved H₂ gas. Animal studies suggest possible antioxidant effects; human evidence is preliminary and inconsistent.
- **"Structured" water** — invokes physics that physics does not support. Avoid.

None of these are scams in the sense of being dangerous. They're just paying-for-marketing categories. If you want clean water, you want certified filtration.

## Whole-house vs. point-of-use

A common question: do I need to treat the water at every tap, or just at the kitchen?

For most contaminants — lead, PFAS, arsenic, nitrate, fluoride — the relevant exposure is what you drink and what you cook with. A point-of-use filter at the kitchen sink does the work.

For **chlorine and disinfection byproducts**, there's a reasonable argument for whole-house treatment: chlorinated shower water aerosolizes some chloroform, and skin absorption of chlorine is real. Whether this matters for health at typical exposures is uncertain. A whole-house activated-carbon system addresses this.

For **lead**, whole-house treatment doesn't make sense, because the lead enters the water from your own pipes downstream of the whole-house filter.

## On bottled water

The honest read: bottled water is regulated by FDA, not EPA, and the standards are similar but enforcement is weaker. Most bottled water is just tap water that has been filtered, often less stringently than what a properly-certified home filter delivers. A few specific brands are higher-quality. Many are not.

If you live in an active health-advisory area (boil water notice, post-Flint situation), bottled water bought from a reputable supplier is a reasonable short-term answer. As a permanent solution, it's expensive, environmentally costly, and not necessarily safer than properly filtered tap.
