## Why "children" gets its own category

For most environmental health questions, framing the answer for adults and adjusting for body weight does a reasonable job. For water, that is *not* enough — and the reasons matter.

1. **Children drink more water per pound.** A nursing infant ingests roughly 150 mL of fluid per kilogram of body weight per day. A toddler drinks proportionally about 3–4 times more water per kilogram than an adult.
2. **Developing organ systems are more vulnerable to specific contaminants.** The developing brain, especially in the first three years, is uniquely sensitive to lead, methylmercury, and possibly fluoride above certain thresholds. The endocrine system is unfolding through puberty — disrupting it during critical windows has consequences adults rarely experience.
3. **Behavior matters.** Toddlers mouth surfaces. Kids drink from garden hoses. Adolescents drink from filtered or bottled sources less consistently.
4. **Formula is the entire diet.** For an exclusively bottle-fed infant, tap water is not part of the diet — it *is* the diet. There is no margin.

This article walks the contaminants where children's exposure or sensitivity differs meaningfully from adults — and what reasonable parental decisions look like at each life stage.

## In utero: pregnancy

Maternal exposure becomes fetal exposure with limited filtration. The contaminants of greatest concern during pregnancy:

- **Lead.** Crosses the placenta. Maternal blood lead — even from lead stored in bone during the mother's own childhood — mobilizes during pregnancy and reaches the fetus. Linked to preterm birth, reduced fetal growth, and reduced infant cognitive performance.
- **PFAS.** Demonstrated placental transfer. Associated with reduced birth weight, pregnancy-induced hypertension, pre-eclampsia, and (more contested) modest reductions in subsequent vaccine antibody response.
- **Nitrate.** Linked to neural tube defects (the strength of this evidence is moderate) and modest associations with preterm birth.
- **Disinfection byproducts (TTHMs, HAAs).** Some evidence of increased miscarriage and small-for-gestational-age outcomes; the evidence is suggestive but not definitive.

**Practical pregnancy guidance:** if you have any of (1) a lead service line or pre-1986 home, (2) a known PFAS-impacted area, (3) agricultural runoff exposure with nitrate above ~5 mg/L, or (4) a surface-water utility with high TTHM history — switching to a properly certified filter for cooking and drinking water is a defensible precautionary step. The cost is low; the regret risk in the other direction is non-trivial.

## Birth to 6 months: the formula window

Exclusively formula-fed infants drink water as roughly 85% of their daily intake. Nothing else in the diet is anywhere near the consumed volume.

The single most consequential decision: **do not mix formula with unfiltered first-draw tap water in a home with a known or unknown lead service line.** Use cold water that has run for at least 2 minutes, or — better — filtered water from an NSF 53–certified filter.

Nitrate at levels above ~10 mg/L can cause methemoglobinemia ("blue baby syndrome") in this age range — the iron in hemoglobin is oxidized to a form unable to carry oxygen. This is acutely dangerous and can be fatal. **In any agricultural region with documented nitrate in source water, never mix formula with unfiltered tap water. Boiling concentrates nitrate; it does not remove it.**

For breastfed infants: the mother's water becomes the infant's via breast milk for most water-soluble contaminants. PFAS in particular concentrates in breast milk relative to maternal serum. This is not generally a reason not to breastfeed — the evidence still strongly favors breastfeeding's net benefits — but it is a reason for the mother to drink well-filtered water during the breastfeeding window.

## 6 months to 5 years: the early childhood window

This is the window during which lead exposure is most consequential. The reason is part biological (the developing brain is rapidly building synapses) and part behavioral (toddlers ingest more dust and surface material).

Lead exposure at this stage:

- Associations with subsequent IQ are strongest for exposure between roughly 12 and 36 months
- The CDC blood lead reference value (3.5 µg/dL) is set conservatively because the dose-response slope is steepest at the low end
- Once exposure ends, blood lead drops, but **the cognitive effects are largely irreversible**

This is also the window where fluoride toothpaste swallowing is the dominant contributor to dental fluorosis. The AAP/ADA guidance: rice-grain smear under age 3, pea-sized amount age 3–6, supervise brushing.

Disinfection byproducts: less obviously a child-specific issue, but worth noting that chloroform inhalation during baths and showers is a low-grade exposure. Whether this matters at typical levels is uncertain.

## 6 to 12 years

Water consumption per kilogram is now declining toward adult levels, but several specific exposures remain notable:

- **School water.** Schools built before 1990 often have lead-bearing plumbing, lead solder, or lead-bearing fixtures that received intermittent use over weekends and summers. First-draw water from a Monday-morning fountain can be much higher in lead than the same water at home that night. The 2024 LCRI accelerates lead-service-line replacement, but in-school plumbing remains the responsibility of the building owner.
- **Hormonal precursors.** Cumulative exposure to endocrine-active compounds (some pesticides, some PFAS) during the years leading up to puberty is one of the strongest reasons for cautious parental filtering, though the evidence on tap-water-level exposures is suggestive rather than conclusive.

If your child's school has not published a lead testing summary, ask. Many states now require it; many districts still don't share results clearly.

## Adolescence

By adolescence the contaminant-per-pound math has converged with adult exposures. The new variable: behavior. Adolescents drink less filtered water (more soda, more sport drinks, more bottled), so any household-level filtration investment has its smallest direct effect on this age group.

A useful default: keep an obvious cold pitcher of properly filtered water visible in the fridge. Behavior follows convenience.

## What about pet children?

Often-overlooked: dogs and cats drink the same household tap water as their humans, often at higher daily volumes per kilogram than adult humans. A 60-pound dog drinks roughly 60 ounces of water per day; over the dog's 12-year lifespan, that's a meaningful cumulative exposure to fluoride, chlorine, and any PFAS in the system. Veterinary toxicology on this is thin — we are not aware of strong evidence that household tap water at U.S. compliance levels is meaningfully harming pets — but if you've installed an RO system, your dog gets the benefit at minimal additional cost.

<Callout title="Practical playbook by stage">
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Pregnancy:</strong> Filter for cooking and drinking. Address lead if pre-1986 home. Address PFAS if in an impacted area.</li>
    <li><strong>0–6 months:</strong> Filtered or bottled water for formula. Never mix formula with unfiltered tap in nitrate or lead-suspect contexts.</li>
    <li><strong>6 mo – 5 yrs:</strong> Lead is the priority. Flush taps, NSF 53 filter, pediatrician blood lead test at age 1 and 2.</li>
    <li><strong>School age:</strong> Ask the school about lead testing. Don't assume "the district is on it."</li>
    <li><strong>Adolescents:</strong> Make filtered water the convenient default at home.</li>
  </ul>
</Callout>
